This study rejects our initial hypothesis that intensive exercise conflicts with sleep time. The large-scale data analysis reveals that students engaging in aerobic exercise 5+ days per week (High group) achieve the highest average sleep 6.93 hours, while sedentary students (Low group) sleep the least (6.67 hours). The One-way ANOVA yielded a highly significant result ($p < 0.05$), confirming that exercise frequency has a robust impact on sleep duration.


Tukey's HSD post-hoc test further demonstrates that the High exercise group is significantly different from both the Low and Medium groups (reject = True). However, the difference between the Low and Medium groups is not statistically significant (reject = False). This implies that only intensive, high-frequency physical exertion triggers a meaningful collective shift in sleep behavior.

These results indicate that teenagers with higher frequencies of intensive aerobic exercise (such as those in dance clubs or school sports teams) experience massive physical exertion, which drives a biological need for more sleep. Even under the realistic constraints of rigid school schedules forcing them to wake up early, they still manage to squeeze out an extra 15 to 20 minutes of sleep compared to their sedentary or less-active peers. Conversely, students who do not exercise may spend more time staying up late scrolling through their phones or playing video games, leading to more severe sleep deprivation.
